The ruins of Langford Tower had been sealed for over three centuries, buried beneath ivy and local superstition. Ethan Cole had no business being there. He was a second-year history major who barely managed to keep his scholarship, not some daring explorer. But Professor Whitmore's extra-credit assignment demanded primary sources, and rumor held that the tower's collapsed cellar still housed documents from the Tudor era.

He squeezed through a gap in the crumbling stone wall, his phone flashlight slicing through curtains of dust. The air tasted of copper and damp earth. Broken steps spiraled downward, and Ethan descended carefully, one hand pressed against the cold wall for balance. At the bottom lay a chamber far larger than any cellar had a right to be. Stone pillars rose into shadow, their surfaces carved with symbols he did not recognize—spirals and crescents interlocking like a forgotten language.

Then he saw it. Resting on a granite pedestal at the room's center was a crystal no bigger than his fist, pulsing with a faint blue glow that seemed to breathe. Ethan reached for it without thinking, the way one reaches toward a campfire on a freezing night, drawn by instinct rather than reason. The moment his fingers closed around the crystal, the glow erupted. Blue light flooded the chamber, the carved symbols blazing to life along every pillar. A low hum vibrated through the floor, through his bones, through the fillings in his teeth.

Images slammed into his mind: a golden throne, a coronation oath spoken in a language he somehow understood, a bloodline stretching back a thousand years—and his own face at the end of it.

The crystal dimmed. Ethan stood alone in the dark, breathing hard, the artifact warm and heavy in his trembling hand. Nothing in his ordinary life had prepared him for a single, impossible truth: the blood of kings ran in his veins.

"Kneel," the Chancellor intoned, his voice echoing.

The Chancellor lowered the crown. It was heavier than Ethan expected, pressing against his temples with a weight that felt less physical and more like responsibility crystallized into metal. A hush fell over the hall, broken only by the soft crackle of candle flame.

"Rise, Your Majesty."

He rose. Two hundred pairs of eyes measured him and found him wanting. But the crown did not fall, and the crystal in his pocket pulsed once—warm, steady, certain. Ethan met their stares and, for the first time, did not look away.
