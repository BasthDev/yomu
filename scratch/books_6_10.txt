Her name was Anneliese Catherine Wyndham, and she had died of typhoid fever in the winter of 1851 at the age of twenty-three. She had been haunting Room 13 ever since, and she had opinions about everything.

"This is unacceptable," she announced at five forty-five the following morning, floating through Jake's closed bedroom door with the casual disregard for privacy that only the dead can achieve. "There is dust on the windowsill. There is a sock — a single sock, not even a pair — draped over the radiator. And the state of your desk can only be described as archaeological."

Jake pulled his pillow over his face. "It's not even six AM."

"Precisely. The day is half-wasted. In my time, a gentleman rose at dawn, attended to his correspondence, and presented himself for breakfast properly attired. You are wearing shorts with cartoon characters on them."

Over the following week, Jake learned several things about his ghostly roommate. First, she could not leave the building — her spectral range ended at the brownstone's front steps. Second, she could move small objects, which she used primarily to rearrange his belongings into configurations she found more aesthetically pleasing. Third, she was absolutely, immovably, magnificently stubborn.

He tried ignoring her. She recited poetry at three in the morning. He tried reasoning with her. She delivered a forty-minute lecture on the decline of modern manners. He tried sleeping with headphones. She stood at the foot of his bed and stared at him until the sheer psychic weight of her disapproval woke him up.

Anneliese Catherine Wyndham was not going anywhere. And neither, unfortunately, was Jake.

Jake blocked the staircase. "You can't do this."

Father Calloway raised an eyebrow. "Young man, I've been doing this for thirty years. The entity will be released to its proper rest. It's really for the best."

Jake thought of Anneliese correcting his history essays. He thought of her floating by the window each evening, watching the street below with an expression that was not sadness exactly, but the memory of sadness — the face of someone who had made peace with her small world and found, against all odds, a kind of contentment within it.

The argument lasted forty-five minutes and involved threats of lease termination, a brief theological debate, and Jake offering to pay an additional two hundred dollars a month in rent.
